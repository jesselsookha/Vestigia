# Vestigia — Guide ○ B  
◆ Showcase Structure as Meaningful Pattern

## Ⅰ — What This Document Is (and Is Not)

This document describes **common structural patterns** found in strong final‑year
showcases, summaries, and portfolio artefacts.

It is **not**:
- a template to fill in
- a required structure
- a submission format
- a grading rubric

You are not expected to follow every section described here.

Instead, this document helps you understand:  
◆ what information audiences usually look for    
◇ why certain sections exist  
◈ how structure supports meaning  

Structure should **serve your narrative**, not replace it.

---

## Ⅱ — Starting Principle: Structure Comes After Extraction

You should not be using this document unless you have already:

- completed Guide ○ A  
- worked through Guide ○ B (01–03)  
- extracted decisions, learning, and contribution  

If you skip directly to structure, your showcase will likely feel:
- generic
- descriptive rather than reflective
- disconnected from your actual work

Structure is the final step, not the starting point.

---

## Ⅲ — A Useful Mental Model

Most effective showcases answer **four underlying questions**, even if they use very
different formats:

Ⅰ — *What was this project about?*  
Ⅱ — *Why did it matter (and to whom)?*  
Ⅲ — *What decisions and learning shaped it?*  
Ⅳ — *What came out of it, and what did I contribute?*  

The sections below map to these questions — not to a fixed order.

---

## Ⅳ — Common Structural Elements (Patterns, Not Instructions)

### Ⅳ.Ⅰ — Context & Framing

This element helps the reader understand:  
◆ the problem or opportunity  
◇ the setting or domain  
◈ constraints or goals  

It is not a full introduction or background chapter.

Effective framing answers:  
- Why does this project exist?  
- What situation or need triggered it?  
- What constraints shaped it?  

This often draws from:
- proposal introductions  
- early Guide ○ A direction records  
- stakeholder or user context  

---

### Ⅳ.Ⅱ — Intent, Role, and Scope

This element clarifies:  
◆ what *you* set out to do  
◇ how your role fit within a group (if applicable)  
◈ what was inside and outside your scope  

Especially important for:  
- group projects
- interdisciplinary work
- externally sponsored or WIL projects  

This section protects integrity by preventing:  
- over‑claiming
- under‑explaining
- ambiguity about contribution

---

### Ⅳ.Ⅲ — Approach & Key Decisions

This is often the **intellectual core** of a strong showcase.

It highlights:  
◆ major decisions  
◇ trade‑offs  
◈ reasoning under uncertainty  

This draws heavily from:  
- Guide ○ A decision records
- design or methodological sections of the project
- problem‑resolution moments

It is usually *selective*, not exhaustive.

---

### Ⅳ.Ⅳ — Challenges & Learning

This element demonstrates growth.

It focuses on:  
◆ problems that mattered  
◇ moments where understanding changed  
◈ how difficulty improved the outcome  

This is *not* a list of failures.

It is an explanation of:  
- how you adapted
- how you learned
- how your thinking matured

This is where your Vestigia records are most visible.

---

### Ⅳ.Ⅴ — Outcomes & Evidence

This element anchors the narrative in reality.

It may include:  
- artefacts
- results
- prototypes
- designs
- evaluations
- deployed systems
- interventions

The key question here is:  
> *What tangible evidence supports the story I am telling?*

This section often links outward rather than reprinting everything.

---

### Ⅳ.Ⅵ — Reflection & Forward View

This element looks beyond the project itself.

It may address:  
◆ what you would do differently  
◇ how this work influenced you  
◈ how skills or perspectives changed  
◉ where this experience leads next  

This reflection is strongest when it:  
- connects directly to earlier decisions  
- avoids generic statements  
- respects context and constraints  

---

## Ⅴ — Ordering and Adaptation

You are not required to follow the order listed above.

In practice:  
- technical projects often foreground decisions
- design‑led projects foreground intent and iteration
- research‑led projects foreground methods and learning

What matters is that:  
◆ the reader can follow your reasoning  
◇ transitions are clear  
◈ structure supports explanation  

---

## Ⅵ — Structure for Different Uses

The same extracted content may be:
- expanded for a portfolio
- condensed for a LinkedIn post
- adapted for a CV link
- discussed in an interview

Strong structure allows **scaling**, not rewriting.

---

## Ⅶ — Final Integrity Check

Before finalising structure, ask:  

◆ Does each section earn its place?    
◇ Does structure clarify or distract?    
◈ Could a reader explain my project back to me accurately?    

If structure becomes the focus, step back.  

Meaning comes first.

---

## Ⅷ — In Summary

Structure is not a checklist.

It is an **organising logic** that helps others understand:  
- what you worked on  
- why it mattered  
- how you thought  
- what you learned  

Guide ○ B teaches you to build structure **from insight**, not habit.
