# Guide B — Extraction Prompts
## Questions and Integrity Checks for Your Showcase

---

## Ⅰ — How to Use This Document

This document is not something you complete from top to bottom.

It is a reference you consult deliberately — returning to different sections
as your extraction work develops. Use it alongside your Vestigia records and
your project artefacts.

The prompts here are designed to help you interrogate your material, not to
generate content mechanically. Some will apply directly to your project.
Some may not apply at all. If a prompt does not open up anything useful, skip it.

If a prompt makes you pause — if it raises a question you are not sure how to
answer — pay attention to that pause. It usually means something worth exploring.

---

## Ⅱ — Before You Begin: The Selection Principle

Hold this principle as you work through every section below:

> Your goal is not to show everything you did.  
> Your goal is to explain what mattered — and why.

Extraction is an act of judgement, not transcription.

---

## Ⅲ — Decision Prompts

Use these to identify decisions worth including in your showcase.

From your records, ask:

◆ Which decisions most significantly shaped what the project became?  
◇ Which decisions required you to choose between two or more credible options?  
◆ Which decisions would raise questions if you left them unexplained?  
◇ Which decisions did you feel least certain about at the time?

For each candidate decision, push further:

- What was at stake when this decision was made?  
- What information was incomplete or uncertain at the time?  
- What trade-off did you accept, and what did you give up?  
- What changed in the project as a direct result of this decision?

A decision belongs in your showcase only if it created consequence —
if something about the project is different because of how you decided.

Decisions that had no real alternatives and no real risk are routine.
They do not need to be there.

---

## Ⅳ — Learning Prompts

Learning is often invisible until you look for it specifically.

From your Vestigia records, ask:

◆ Where in my records did my understanding shift?  
◇ Which assumptions did I hold at the start that I later revised or abandoned?  
◆ What did I struggle with early that became clearer by the end?  
◇ Which entries show a noticeably different level of understanding than earlier ones?

From your project artefacts, ask:

- Where is the learning visible in the output?  
- What improved — technically, methodologically, or analytically — as the project progressed?  
- What would have been weaker or different if I had built it in the first month instead of the last?

Learning is not a catalogue of tools you used or skills you practised.

It is **change over time** — a before and an after that you can point to
in both your records and your work.

---

## Ⅴ — Problem and Resolution Prompts

Do not hide difficulty. Do not dramatise it either.

The aim is to identify problems that produced insight — not every obstacle,
but the ones that genuinely changed the project or changed you.

From your records, ask:

◆ Which problems forced a change in approach?  
◇ Which failures led to better decisions later?  
◆ Which obstacles reshaped your plan, scope, or design?

For each problem you identify, connect it forward:

- What exactly went wrong, and when?  
- Why did the original approach fail?  
- What did you modify, rethink, or replace as a result?  
- How did the final outcome benefit from how you handled it?

A problem without a resolution or learning outcome does not belong in your
public narrative. Include difficulty because it demonstrates growth — not
because it demonstrates that the project was hard.

---

## Ⅵ — Contribution Prompts

These prompts are essential if your project was collaborative.

Your showcase is individual. You need to be able to distinguish your
contribution from the collective work of the team — honestly and specifically.

From your Vestigia records, ask:

◆ What did I initiate rather than inherit from the group?  
◇ What did I take responsibility for from start to completion?  
◆ Where did my decisions visibly influence the group's direction?  
◇ What would be incomplete or different if I had not been involved?

From your project artefacts, ask:

- Which components reflect my direct input?  
- Where is my contribution traceable in the deliverable?  
- Which documentation sections did I author or significantly shape?

Integrity check:

- Could I explain this contribution clearly and specifically in an interview?  
- Would a teammate recognise this description as accurate and fair?  
- Am I describing ownership, or am I describing involvement?

The goal is **defensible contribution** — not exclusivity, not false modesty.

---

## Ⅶ — Audience Alignment Prompts

Your showcase will be read by people who were not part of your project.

Before structuring anything, consider your audience:

◆ What would a reader outside my institution need to understand this work?  
◇ What background knowledge am I assuming they have?  
◆ Where am I describing what I did without explaining why?  
◇ Which technical or discipline-specific terms require a sentence of context?

Then refine:

- Which details add clarity and which create noise?  
- Is the project's purpose clear from the first paragraph?  
- Would someone reading this in a different field understand what the project was about?

Accessibility is not the same as simplicity. You do not need to remove depth —
you need to make the depth accessible to someone coming in fresh.

---

## Ⅷ — Integrity Checks

Before you finalise what you plan to present, run through these checks.
Answer them honestly — not as you wish things were, but as they are.

◆ Does this accurately represent what happened?  
◇ Is anything misleading through omission, framing, or emphasis?  
◆ Am I presenting growth and honest engagement — not a performance of success?  
◇ Have I acknowledged collaborators in a way that is fair and specific?

Ask yourself:

> *Would I be comfortable defending every claim here — verbally, to someone who
> knows the project?*

If the answer is uncertain about anything, revise that part before proceeding.

This is not excessive caution. It is professional habit.

---

## Ⅸ — What Not to Extract

It is worth being explicit about what does not belong in a showcase.

Leave out:

◆ routine activity with no decision or learning value  
◇ work done entirely by others that you cannot speak to directly  
◆ repeated struggles that produced no insight or resolution  
◇ raw notes that require extensive explanation to be understood  
◆ anything you cannot confidently defend if questioned

Exclusion is not dishonesty. It is discernment.

A showcase is not a complete project diary. It is a curated account of your
most meaningful engagement with the work. What you leave out serves the same
purpose as what you keep: it keeps the narrative clear and the claims defensible.

---

## Ⅹ — When the Prompts Have Done Their Work

When you have worked through the relevant prompts and applied them to your
material, you should have:

◆ a small set of decisions you can explain fully and specifically  
◇ a clear account of how your learning changed across the project  
◆ at least one honest problem-resolution sequence that demonstrates growth  
◇ a specific, defensible account of your individual contribution  
◆ evidence linked to real artefacts from the project

Only at this point should you begin thinking about format, structure, and presentation.

Structure serves meaning. If you build structure before you have extracted meaning,
the structure will be empty — and it will show.
